How Disease Progression Affects the Body

As IPF scarring and honeycombs continue to form, you will begin to feel their effects on your body, including symptoms like:


  • shortness of breath

  • a dry, hacking cough

  • rapid, shallow breathing

  • fatigue

  • gradual weight loss

  • aching muscles and joints

  • widening and rounding of the fingertips or toes

Symptoms may worsen to the point that they take a toll on your quality of life—from your emotional state to your ability to complete everyday tasks.

“I began having trouble doing simple things. I could shop for my groceries without any problem, but bringing them in the house left me huffing and puffing. I needed to rest before I could put my groceries away. Any type of exercise that required a little more effort caused me to lose my breath.”


— Gene, PERSON LIVING WITH IPF

Pulmonologists will track IPF’s effect on the body with certain tests , like HRCT scans, forced vital capacity (FVC), and walking tests.


While these tests can help provide a clearer picture of how IPF is progressing, remember that one single test may not tell the whole story. Even if one test result remains the same, IPF may still be progressing.


Additionally, it’s important to understand that test results aren’t just numbers on a chart—they may help explain why you’re feeling a certain way.


For example, FVC is related to your ability to be physically active. One study found that a 1% loss in FVC equates to 60 fewer steps per day. In other words, test results could help explain or predict the impact of IPF on your daily life.

HELPFUL HINT

Tests used to detect IPF progression include:

  • HRCT scans
  • Pulse oximetry (measured at rest and with exertion)
  • Exercise/walking tests
  • Spirometry (forced vital capacity [FVC])
  • DLCO
  • Lung volume measurement (total lung capacity [TLC])
  • Arterial blood gas testing
  • Questionnaire, like the University of California San Diego Shortness of Breath Questionnaire

HELPFUL HINT

Forced vital capacity (FVC) is a measure of the amount of air you exhale after you take a deep breath and blow out as hard as possible.
