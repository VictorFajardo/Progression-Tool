Scarring Progresses

As time goes on, the abnormal “healing” process creates more and more thick scar tissue in the alveoli walls and the spaces around the alveoli.

This thickening makes it difficult for oxygen to move across the walls of the alveoli and into blood vessels, called capillaries, that provide oxygen-rich blood to the rest of the body.

When this happens, the body’s organs, like the heart and the brain, do not receive the oxygen they need to function properly.

As IPF progresses, sheets of dense scar tissue replace normal lung tissue and shrink the size of the lung.

Some areas of the lung may be replaced by thick-walled cystic airspaces called “honeycombs,” which may be seen on HRCT scans.


Honeycombing means there is complete destruction of healthy lung tissue, and the areas of the lung where honeycombing forms no longer allow oxygen to enter the bloodstream. Honeycombing and scarring of the lungs result in shortness of breath, coughing, and other symptoms of IPF.

When IPF first begins, you may not experience any symptoms, like shortness of breath and coughing. Therefore, it’s important to remember that symptoms are not the only indication of what’s occurring inside your body.


Even without symptoms present, IPF may still be progressing and causing irreparable damage to your lungs.


Additionally, progression doesn’t always show up on HRCT scans, which is why it’s important that other tests are performed.
